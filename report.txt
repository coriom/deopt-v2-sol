# DeOpt v2 Architecture Scalability Diagnostic

Scope: architecture scalability, modularity, extensibility, and production-grade maintainability.

Assumption: v1 production launch means the current European options + perpetuals scope described in SPEC.md and ARCHITECTURE_MAP.md. "Future protocol expansion" means futures, structured products, additional engines, broader collateral, per-product/per-market fees, richer risk modules, and institutional-grade configuration.

## 1. product extensibility gaps

### Finding: Product model is split by current engine type rather than generalized product type

- severity: important
- why it matters: DeOpt currently has an options registry and a perp market registry, but no product-type abstraction that lets futures, spreads, exotics, structured notes, or future engines register themselves under a common product lifecycle, account-risk, fee, pause, settlement, and indexing contract. Adding each new derivative family will likely require a new registry, new matching engine surface, new risk integration, new governance wrappers, and custom observability.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`, `src/margin/*`, `src/perp/*`, `src/matching/*`, `src/risk/IRiskModule.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/gouvernance/*`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Introduce a protocol-level product/engine registry in a later version that maps `productType` and `productId` to engine, risk adapter, fee policy, collateral policy, settlement policy, lifecycle status, and metadata. Keep current option/perp registries as v1 adapters rather than replacing them immediately.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Options product assumptions are hard-coded around European vanilla options

- severity: useful
- why it matters: `OptionProductRegistry.OptionSeries` supports call/put, expiry, strike, European flag, settlement asset, and a hard-locked `contractSize1e8 == 1e8`. This is safe for v1, but it does not naturally represent spreads, baskets, barriers, digitals, volatility products, variable contract sizes, or custom payoff formulas without overloading metadata or adding parallel registries.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineStorage.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineOps.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleMargin.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Preserve the locked v1 option series type, but define a future `IProductSpec` or `IPayoffAdapter` interface for non-vanilla products. Do not loosen the v1 contract-size invariant in-place; instead route custom products through new adapters with explicit unit and payoff declarations.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Matching ingress is product-specific rather than execution-protocol specific

- severity: important
- why it matters: Options and perps have separate matching engines with different EIP-712 payloads and engine call surfaces. This is workable for two products, but additional engines would replicate nonce, executor, pause, signature, and settlement-forwarding logic. Institutional execution also often needs order families, RFQ, block trades, portfolio trades, and multi-leg atomic execution.
- affected files/modules: `src/matching/MatchingEngine.sol`, `src/matching/PerpMatchingEngine.sol`, `src/matching/IMarginEngineTrade.sol`, `src/matching/IPerpEngineTrade.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Keep v1 matching engines stable, then add a versioned execution router with common nonce/signature controls and product-specific payload adapters. Multi-leg or structured-product execution should settle atomically through product adapters rather than duplicating matching engines.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Product lifecycle states are not normalized across products

- severity: useful
- why it matters: Options use series active/inactive, settlement proposal/finality, settlement pause, and engine-level emergency close-only. Perps use market active/close-only, activation state, funding state, and engine-level emergency close-only. Future products will need consistent state meanings for active, restricted, close-only, settlement-only, expired, paused, deprecated, and delisted.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineAdmin.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Define a shared lifecycle enum and transition policy for future product registries. Current v1 flags can be mapped into the shared lifecycle view without changing existing behavior.
- estimated complexity: medium

## 2. risk-module extensibility gaps

### Finding: Unified risk aggregation is hard-coded to options plus one perp risk module

- severity: important
- why it matters: `IRiskModule.ProductRiskState` and `computeDetailedAccountRisk` expose fixed options/perps fields and allocate exactly two product contributions. This gives useful v1 observability, but it does not scale to futures, structured products, multiple perp engines, isolated-margin products, portfolio-margin engines, or third-party risk adapters without changing the interface and downstream consumers.
- affected files/modules: `src/risk/IRiskModule.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleStorage.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/perp/PerpRiskModule.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a future risk-adapter registry where each product engine exposes a standard account contribution interface returning equity delta, IM, MM, funding, liability, bad debt, and confidence/staleness metadata. Preserve current fixed views as v1 compatibility wrappers.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Risk composition is additive and lacks portfolio-margin dependency modeling

- severity: important
- why it matters: Current architecture aggregates option margin and perp margin with additive contributions. Long-term institutional products need correlation offsets, cross-greeks, basis risk, spread recognition, per-product isolation modes, and stress scenario aggregation across products. Pure additive composition is conservative but capital-inefficient and becomes hard to evolve once many engines exist.
- affected files/modules: `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleMargin.sol`, `src/perp/PerpRiskModule.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineViews.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Introduce a versioned risk model layer that separates product-local risk from portfolio aggregation. Start with conservative additive v1 adapters, then add governed portfolio-margin models with explicit enablement per account/product/market.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Options risk policy is per-underlying, not per-product or per-series

- severity: useful
- why it matters: `OptionRiskConfig` is keyed by underlying. That is suitable for vanilla ETH/BTC options, but custom tenors, expiries, strikes, settlement assets, volatility regimes, and structured products may need separate risk curves or stress policies. Per-underlying policy cannot express differentiated treatment for weekly options, long-dated options, illiquid strikes, or product-specific payoff risk.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleMargin.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add optional risk-policy IDs for future products/series while preserving per-underlying defaults. Resolution order should be series/product policy, then underlying policy, then protocol fallback.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Risk module uses best-effort external integration paths that become ambiguous at scale

- severity: important
- why it matters: Unified risk currently uses optional/best-effort calls for perp risk, PnL, funding, and bad debt. This is practical for incremental integration, but with multiple risk adapters, silent omission or adapter failure can make account risk appear incomplete unless each contribution carries explicit availability and failure status.
- affected files/modules: `src/risk/RiskModuleStorage.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/perp/PerpRiskModule.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: For future adapters, return explicit status fields such as `ok`, `stale`, `unavailable`, `conservativeFallbackUsed`, and `blocking`. Account-risk computation should fail closed or include a visible degraded-mode flag rather than silently omitting product risk.
- estimated complexity: medium

## 3. collateral extensibility gaps

### Finding: There are two collateral policy sources that can diverge

- severity: important
- why it matters: `CollateralVault` stores token support, decimals, and `collateralFactorBps`; `RiskModule` stores separate `collateralConfigs`; `PerpRiskModule` reads vault collateral factors directly. This can produce inconsistent collateral valuation across unified risk, perp-only risk, seizer planning, and withdraw previews if governance updates one surface but not another.
- affected files/modules: `src/collateral/CollateralVaultStorage.sol`, `src/collateral/CollateralVaultAdmin.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleStorage.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleCollateral.sol`, `src/perp/PerpRiskModule.sol`, `src/liquidation/CollateralSeizer.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no, if deployment/configuration procedures enforce synchronization
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Create a single collateral policy registry or a canonical read-only collateral policy interface consumed by all risk and liquidation modules. Preserve vault token support as custody configuration, but source risk weights, launch activation, and product-specific collateral eligibility from one canonical policy layer.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Single base collateral numeraire limits multi-base and institutional collateral models

- severity: important
- why it matters: The protocol correctly standardizes risk into `baseCollateralToken`, but future institutional configurability may need multiple settlement numeraires, subaccounts, isolated books, cross-currency margin, or per-product base assets. A single global base is simpler and safer for v1, but it constrains expansion toward multi-collateral credit portfolios.
- affected files/modules: `src/risk/IRiskModule.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleStorage.sol`, `src/perp/PerpRiskModule.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineStorage.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineStorage.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Keep global base collateral for v1. For expansion, introduce account/book-level risk domains where each domain declares base asset, collateral universe, risk adapters, and settlement policy. Avoid migrating v1 base semantics in place.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Collateral eligibility is global, not product/market-specific

- severity: important
- why it matters: The current vault can restrict launch-active collateral and risk modules apply weights, but collateral eligibility does not appear to be resolved per product, market, account tier, or risk domain. Futures, exotics, and institutional accounts often need collateral rules that vary by market liquidity, settlement asset, counterparty class, and liquidation path.
- affected files/modules: `src/collateral/CollateralVaultStorage.sol`, `src/collateral/CollateralVaultAdmin.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleCollateral.sol`, `src/perp/PerpRiskModule.sol`, `src/liquidation/CollateralSeizer.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add per-product or per-risk-domain collateral policy overrides. Resolution should be explicit: product/market policy, then risk-domain default, then global fallback. Disabled collateral should remain withdrawable but non-contributing.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Liquidation collateral ordering and seizure policy are not generalized by market context

- severity: useful
- why it matters: `CollateralSeizer` plans seizure from the configured collateral universe using token-level seize config and base-first behavior. As collateral and product count grows, liquidation policy may need product-specific collateral ordering, auction eligibility, slippage models, liquidity haircuts, and settlement-asset preference.
- affected files/modules: `src/liquidation/CollateralSeizer.sol`, `src/liquidation/ICollateralSeizer.sol`, `src/collateral/CollateralVaultViews.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Keep v1 deterministic base-first seizure. For expansion, add a policy ID to liquidation requests so the seizer can resolve market/product-specific collateral ordering and discounts without changing vault accounting.
- estimated complexity: medium

## 4. fee-system extensibility gaps

### Finding: Fee profiles are trader-level and role-level, not product/market-level

- severity: important
- why it matters: `FeesManager` resolves default, tier, or override fees by trader and maker/taker role only. Per-product/per-market fees, instrument-specific fees, launch promotions, risk-based fees, settlement fees, liquidation fees, and institutional schedules cannot be expressed directly. Engines must supply premium and notional, but policy selection lacks market context.
- affected files/modules: `src/fees/FeesManager.sol`, `src/fees/IFeesManager.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineStorage.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineStorage.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Introduce a versioned fee quote interface that accepts context: product type, market/series ID, settlement asset, account, role, notional, premium, order type, and optional fee policy ID. Keep current `quoteFee` as the v1 default adapter.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Integer-bps fee precision is too coarse for institutional fee schedules

- severity: useful
- why it matters: `uint16` basis-point fees cannot represent sub-bps schedules precisely, and current tier profiles floor or approximate fractional bps. This is acceptable for a simple v1, but high-volume derivatives markets commonly require sub-bps maker/taker fees, rebates, and negotiated institutional fee schedules.
- affected files/modules: `src/fees/FeesManager.sol`, `src/fees/IFeesManager.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a future fee policy with higher precision, such as parts-per-million or 1e8 rates, while preserving BPS invariants for existing fields. Do not reinterpret existing `...Bps` values.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Fee routing is engine-local rather than policy-driven

- severity: useful
- why it matters: Fee recipients are set on engines and fees are computed separately by `FeesManager`. Future per-market treasuries, insurance allocation, referrer splits, maker rebates, protocol revenue sharing, and product-specific fee sinks will require routing logic beyond a single recipient per engine.
- affected files/modules: `src/margin/MarginEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineStorage.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineStorage.sol`, `src/fees/FeesManager.sol`, `src/core/InsuranceFund.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a fee router or fee settlement policy that computes both amount and destinations. Engine-level fee recipient can remain a fallback for v1.
- estimated complexity: medium

## 5. registry/configuration architecture gaps

### Finding: Configuration is distributed across many owner-controlled modules without a canonical config graph

- severity: important
- why it matters: Product, risk, collateral, oracle, fee, insurance, matching, and emergency controls are configured in separate contracts. This is modular, but large-scale operation needs a single inspectable configuration graph to answer whether a product is launch-ready, fully wired, safe, and governed. Without that, deployment and audits rely on offchain runbooks.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`, `src/collateral/CollateralVaultAdmin.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleAdmin.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/fees/FeesManager.sol`, `src/oracle/OracleRouter.sol`, `src/core/InsuranceFund.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no, if deployment readiness is externally checked
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a read-only protocol configuration registry/checker that records canonical module addresses, dependency wiring, policy IDs, and readiness status per market/product. It should not own economic logic; it should make configuration auditable and machine-readable.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Governance wrappers enumerate known module targets and do not scale to arbitrary engines

- severity: important
- why it matters: `RiskGovernor` stores fixed target addresses for risk, margin, oracle, fees, option registry, vault, insurance, perp registry, and perp engine. Queue helpers are generated around known interfaces. Future engines can still be governed through generic `queueOperation`, but they lose typed helper coverage and operational readability unless the governor is modified.
- affected files/modules: `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorStorage.sol`, `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorAdmin.sol`, `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorInterfaces.sol`, `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorQueue.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a target registry keyed by module type and module ID for future governance surfaces. Keep generic timelock execution as the final authority, but make helper generation/adapters data-driven or modular.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Metadata fields are too small to carry institutional configuration context

- severity: useful
- why it matters: Options and perp registries expose `bytes32` metadata fields. This is useful for compact labels or hashes, but not enough for rich product terms, legal docs, risk-model IDs, market-maker program IDs, settlement calendars, or offchain schema references unless conventions are added externally.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: no, but it limits operational clarity
- recommended implementation approach: Standardize metadata as content-addressed hashes or typed policy IDs. For future products, emit structured metadata events and expose schema/version fields rather than relying on opaque `bytes32` alone.
- estimated complexity: low

### Finding: Deployment/configuration support is not first-class in the repository

- severity: important
- why it matters: The `script` directory currently appears empty. Production and future multi-engine deployments need deterministic wiring scripts, environment-specific parameter manifests, post-deploy readiness checks, and governance handoff scripts. Without these, scalability depends on manual configuration discipline.
- affected files/modules: `script/`, `DEPLOYMENT_PLAN.md`, `PARAMETERS.md`, module admin contracts
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: yes, if no external deployment automation exists
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add deterministic deploy/configure/verify scripts that emit the full module graph, configure parameters from versioned manifests, validate invariants, and produce governance-ready transactions. Keep scripts protocol-facing and avoid changing core contracts for this.
- estimated complexity: medium

## 6. governance scalability gaps

### Finding: Timelock has one global delay instead of action-risk tiers

- severity: useful
- why it matters: `ProtocolTimelock` enforces one `minDelay` for queued operations. As the protocol grows, low-risk operational updates, high-risk economic changes, emergency actions, and product listings may require different review windows. A single delay is simple and safe, but can be either too slow for operations or too fast for major risk changes.
- affected files/modules: `src/gouvernance/ProtocolTimelock.sol`, `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorQueue.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: no, but it limits governance maturity
- recommended implementation approach: Keep one delay for v1. For expansion, introduce action categories or target/function-level delay policies enforced by timelock or governor, with longer delays for economic and upgrade-sensitive changes.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Guardian powers are module-local and not coordinated through a shared incident policy

- severity: important
- why it matters: Many modules have guardian pause powers, but each module defines its own emergency flags and owner-only recovery. This gives useful granularity, but coordinated incidents across oracle, collateral, matching, risk, settlement, and liquidation require an operator to know which flags to set in which order. Future products increase this operational burden.
- affected files/modules: `src/collateral/CollateralVaultAdmin.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleAdmin.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/fees/FeesManager.sol`, `src/oracle/OracleRouter.sol`, `src/core/InsuranceFund.sol`, `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no, if runbooks exist
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add an incident-response registry or playbook contract/view that groups emergency actions by incident type. Avoid centralizing all pause authority blindly; instead provide auditable bundles and readiness views.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: New engine onboarding requires governance contract updates or generic opaque operations

- severity: important
- why it matters: Fixed governance helper surfaces are understandable for v1 but do not scale cleanly as more engines and registries are added. Relying only on generic calldata operations reduces readability and increases operational risk for institutional governance.
- affected files/modules: `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorStorage.sol`, `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorQueue.sol`, `src/gouvernance/RiskGovernorInterfaces.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: For expansion, use modular governor adapters or a governed module registry. Each new engine should publish a typed governance adapter that can be registered without changing the core governor storage layout.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Configuration changes lack protocol-wide dependency validation before execution

- severity: important
- why it matters: Governance can update oracle, vault, risk, fee, seizer, insurance, and matching pointers independently. A single valid module-level update can still leave the overall protocol graph inconsistent, for example risk module not matching vault collateral policy or engine not authorized in vault. This becomes more dangerous with more products.
- affected files/modules: `src/gouvernance/*`, `src/collateral/CollateralVaultAdmin.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleAdmin.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineAdmin.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineAdmin.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no, if deployment checks exist
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a read-only dependency validator used by deployment scripts and governance proposals. For high-risk changes, require proposal bundles to include post-state validation calls or offchain preflight artifacts.
- estimated complexity: medium

## 7. observability/indexing scalability gaps

### Finding: Rich views are useful but still fixed to v1 product taxonomy

- severity: useful
- why it matters: Recent observability additions expose detailed account risk, liquidation previews, settlement previews, and bad-debt events. However, `DetailedAccountRisk` still returns exactly options and perps contributions. Indexers and risk dashboards will need a generalized contribution model before additional engines can be displayed consistently.
- affected files/modules: `src/risk/IRiskModule.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineViews.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineViews.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add paginated, dynamic product contribution views keyed by product type/engine/market ID. Keep existing fixed views for v1 compatibility.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Registry enumeration is present but not optimized for very large product sets

- severity: useful
- why it matters: Registries expose arrays and slice helpers, which is good for v1. With many expiries, strikes, products, markets, and custom structures, indexers will need richer filtering by lifecycle state, product type, settlement asset, risk policy, fee policy, and metadata schema.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: no, but it increases indexer complexity
- recommended implementation approach: Add event-complete indexing commitments and selective paginated views for common filters. Prefer events for indexers and bounded views for admin/readiness tooling.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Configuration readiness is not exposed as a consolidated on-chain view

- severity: important
- why it matters: Operators and indexers need to know whether a market is active, close-only, launch-capped, has feeds, has risk policy, has collateral policy, has fee policy, has matching ingress, and has insurance/seizer coverage. Today this is spread across modules, which is manageable for v1 but not scalable for many products.
- affected files/modules: `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineViews.sol`, `src/margin/MarginEngineViews.sol`, `src/oracle/OracleRouter.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no, if external monitoring covers it
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a read-only `ProtocolReadinessLens` or equivalent script/lens that aggregates product readiness without mutating state. Include status flags and missing-dependency reasons.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Event schemas do not yet define a unified indexing standard across engines

- severity: useful
- why it matters: Events are enriched for key v1 flows, but future engines will need consistent event vocabulary for product creation, policy assignment, trade execution, settlement, liquidation, fee routing, collateral use, insurance coverage, and bad debt. Without a standard, indexers need per-engine custom logic.
- affected files/modules: `src/margin/MarginEngineTypes.sol`, `src/perp/PerpEngineTypes.sol`, `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`, `src/fees/IFeesManager.sol`, `src/core/InsuranceFund.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Define a shared event schema for future engines. Keep current events, but require new product adapters to emit common indexed fields such as account, productType, marketId/productId, policyId, asset, amount, and base-value equivalent.
- estimated complexity: medium

## 8. recommended implementation roadmap

### Finding: Phase 1 - Add deployment and configuration readiness tooling before v1 launch

- severity: critical
- why it matters: The most launch-relevant scalability gap is not a contract refactor; it is deterministic wiring, configuration validation, and readiness reporting. The protocol already has many safety controls, but production operation needs proof that each module is connected and parameterized coherently.
- affected files/modules: `script/`, `DEPLOYMENT_PLAN.md`, `PARAMETERS.md`, `src/*Admin.sol`, `src/gouvernance/*`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: yes, if not handled by an external deployment system
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Build deploy/configure/verify scripts and a read-only readiness checklist. Validate module addresses, ownership/timelock handoff, vault engine authorization, collateral policy sync, oracle feeds, launch caps, emergency guardians, fee recipients, insurance callers, and registry activation states.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Phase 2 - Stabilize canonical policy ownership

- severity: important
- why it matters: Collateral, risk, fee, lifecycle, liquidation, and oracle policies are currently distributed across modules. Before adding more products, the protocol should decide which surfaces are canonical and which are execution caches or compatibility views.
- affected files/modules: `src/collateral/*`, `src/risk/*`, `src/perp/PerpRiskModule.sol`, `src/liquidation/*`, `src/fees/*`, `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: First document the canonical source of truth per policy. Then add read-only validators. Only after validation is stable, introduce canonical registries for collateral policy, fee policy, and product/risk policy IDs.
- estimated complexity: medium

### Finding: Phase 3 - Introduce product/risk adapter architecture without replacing v1 engines

- severity: important
- why it matters: The current engines are appropriate for v1, but adding futures or structured products by copy-pasting engine-specific patterns will increase governance, observability, and risk integration complexity. Adapter architecture lets v1 remain stable while future engines plug into common surfaces.
- affected files/modules: `src/risk/IRiskModule.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/matching/*`, `src/gouvernance/*`, future product registries
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Define future interfaces for product metadata, account exposure, risk contribution, lifecycle status, fee context, and liquidation/settlement previews. Wrap current options/perps as adapters. Avoid broad migration until the adapter interfaces are tested.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Phase 4 - Add contextual fee and collateral policies

- severity: important
- why it matters: Per-product/per-market fees and per-product collateral eligibility are explicit future goals. They should be introduced after readiness tooling and canonical policy ownership are clear, otherwise they will multiply configuration drift.
- affected files/modules: `src/fees/*`, `src/collateral/*`, `src/risk/*`, `src/liquidation/*`, `src/margin/*`, `src/perp/*`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add policy IDs to market/product config. Fee quote and collateral contribution paths should resolve contextual policy first, then fall back to global v1 defaults.
- estimated complexity: high

### Finding: Phase 5 - Evolve governance and observability into dynamic module infrastructure

- severity: useful
- why it matters: More engines require governance, monitoring, and indexing to be dynamic rather than hard-coded. This should come after current launch controls are validated, not before.
- affected files/modules: `src/gouvernance/*`, `src/risk/IRiskModule.sol`, `src/risk/RiskModuleViews.sol`, `src/OptionProductRegistry.sol`, `src/perp/PerpMarketRegistry.sol`
- whether it blocks v1 production launch: no
- whether it blocks future protocol expansion: yes
- recommended implementation approach: Add a governed module registry, typed governance adapters, generalized product contribution views, and shared event/indexing standards. Preserve generic timelock execution as the fallback authority.
- estimated complexity: high
